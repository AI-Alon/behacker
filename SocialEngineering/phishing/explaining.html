Phishing Page (Credential Harvesting)

What is it?

Phishing is a social engineering attack where an attacker clones a legitimate login page and hosts it on a lookalike domain. Victims enter their credentials believing they are on the real site. The attacker captures them and redirects the victim to the real site — often without the victim realising anything happened.

Why it works

  • Humans recognize brand logos and page layouts, not URLs
  • URLs are long, truncated on mobile, and easy to obscure
  • Homograph attacks use Unicode characters that look identical to ASCII
  • A valid TLS certificate (padlock icon) does not mean the site is legitimate

Homograph attack examples

  • gîthub.com — real: github.com (Unicode î)
  • paypaI.com — real: paypal.com (uppercase i)
  • аpple.com — real: apple.com (Cyrillic а)
  • arnazon.com — real: amazon.com (rn looks like m)
  • secure-paypal.com — real: paypal.com

Technical implementation

Modern phishing kits:

  • Reverse proxy phishing: Tools like Evilginx2 sit between victim and real site, forwarding all requests and capturing session cookies — bypassing SMS-based 2FA
  • Static clones: HTMLs captured with wget --mirror or HTTrack
  • Credential relay: Captured username/password used immediately against the real site

What attackers capture

  • Username/password → immediate account takeover
  • Session cookies (via proxy) → bypasses MFA
  • 2FA codes → fed in real-time to the real site
  • Security question answers → account recovery takeover

Detection signals for users

  1. Check the full domain in the address bar (not just the lock icon)
  2. Your password manager won't autofill on a different domain
  3. Unexpected email asking you to log in
  4. URL delivered via SMS, messaging app, or email

Phishing-resistant defenses

  • FIDO2/WebAuthn: Hardware keys are cryptographically bound to the origin — they cannot be used on a phishing domain
  • Password managers: Won't autofill on wrong domain
  • Email authentication: SPF, DKIM, DMARC to make spoofing harder
  • User training: Regular simulated phishing campaigns
